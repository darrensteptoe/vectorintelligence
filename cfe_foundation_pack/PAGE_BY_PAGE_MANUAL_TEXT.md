# PAGE_BY_PAGE_MANUAL_TEXT

This file contains the dense, operator-teaching manual copy for the major pages of CFE.

The manual should not read like tooltip fragments stitched together. It should read like a serious operating handbook built into the application. It should help a candidate, finance director, campaign manager, or consultant understand:
- what the surface is for
- what the numbers mean
- what good looks like
- what weak looks like
- what to do next
- what not to overread

The manual can live in the right rail, in expandable teaching cards, or in a dedicated manual view within each page. It should remain page-aware.

---

## 1. Finance Path Dashboard

### Manual title
**Finance Path Dashboard — What the Campaign Must Raise, By When, and Why**

### Manual body
This page is the command surface for the campaign’s funding path.

It is not a ledger, and it is not a history page. Its job is to answer the practical question that finance leadership faces every day: what does this campaign need to have in place financially, when do those dollars need to exist, and are we on pace to cover the plan we are trying to run?

The dashboard combines four things:
1. the campaign’s full budget plan,
2. the timing of when those costs hit,
3. the campaign’s current financial position,
4. and the path required to safely cover the plan.

The first thing to understand is that this page is about timing, not just totals. A campaign can have a respectable overall target and still be in danger if the money is not arriving before staffing ramps, poll commitments, production windows, or field expansion points. That is why the dashboard emphasizes checkpoints, reserve status, and funding pressure by phase rather than only showing a single total raise number.

### What the key cards mean

#### Total Projected Budget
This is the current modeled cost of the campaign plan across all major budget domains. It reflects the selected budget scenario, not merely the amount already spent. If this number changes materially, it usually means the plan changed, the field scenario changed, or a major line was rephased.

A strong operator does not look at this number in isolation. The more important question is whether the campaign is building a path to fund it before the plan demands the cash.

#### Safe Funding Threshold
This is the amount the campaign must realistically secure to avoid starving core operations. It is not necessarily the “likely win” number. It is the line below which the campaign begins to make distorted choices: delayed hiring, skipped infrastructure, compressed persuasion windows, or underfunded turnout work.

#### Competitive Funding Threshold
This is the level at which the campaign is operating within a historically credible band for this race type and strategy. It does not guarantee success, but it means the campaign is less likely to be structurally underfunded.

#### Likely Funding Band
This is a range, not a promise. It is informed by historical analogs and by the campaign’s actual plan. When the campaign is below this range, it may still compete, but it is likely doing so with less margin for error. When it is above this range, the question becomes whether the additional budget is actually improving performance or simply increasing overhead and complexity.

#### Reserve Status
Reserve status exists to answer a question campaigns often avoid until too late: if revenue slowed for a period or an expected commitment slipped, could the campaign still protect the plan without immediate damage?

Green reserve status means the campaign currently has enough cushion to absorb reasonable stress. Caution means the plan may still be viable, but the campaign is operating with reduced flexibility. Redline means leadership should assume that a missed event, delayed deposit, or early expenditure could force operational compromise.

#### Gap to Safe Funding / Gap to Competitive Funding
These are the practical gap measures. They should shape behavior more than the vanity of a large gross target. The point is not simply to admire the gap; it is to understand what must close it, by when, and through which channels.

### How to read the path status
The path status is not a mood indicator. It is a management signal.

- **On Path** means the campaign’s current pace and upcoming program are consistent with the modeled requirement.
- **Narrow Margin** means the campaign is still viable under current assumptions, but there is limited cushion.
- **Behind Path** means the current pace is insufficient relative to the plan’s timing.
- **Redline** means leadership should assume the active plan is not cash-safe unless corrective action occurs quickly.

### What good looks like
Good does not mean every card is green forever. Good means:
- the campaign understands its next checkpoint,
- reserve status is protected before large commitments,
- the gap is being closed through real activity rather than hope,
- and the campaign can explain, in operational terms, why the next dollars matter.

### What weak looks like
Weak campaigns often show the same pattern:
- respectable gross number with weak reserve status,
- activity completion below plan,
- too much dependence on one event or one donor cluster,
- and no clear answer to what has to happen next week.

### Practical guidance
If this page is yellow or red, do not start by arguing with the model. Start by asking:
- Did the budget expand without a finance adjustment?
- Did a key channel underperform?
- Is the field or staff ramp happening before the money is in?
- Are we counting pledges as if they were already liquid?
- Are we overestimating event or online performance?

The dashboard is most useful when it forces concrete next actions.

## 2. Budget Builder

### Manual title
**Budget Builder — Building the Campaign the Finance Plan Must Actually Support**

### Manual body
This page exists to solve a basic problem that undermines a lot of campaigns: they talk about fundraising goals without first defining what the campaign is trying to fund.

The Budget Builder is where the campaign translates strategy into a costed plan. If the campaign intends to run field, digital, mail, payroll, consultants, event infrastructure, and late persuasion work, all of that needs to appear here in some structured form. Otherwise, the finance plan is built on partial truth.

This page should be approached as a planning discipline, not an exercise in fantasy precision. The goal is not to pretend the campaign can know every future invoice down to the dollar. The goal is to define the major cost structure, phase it appropriately, and distinguish between the core plan and optional expansions.

### What each budget line should capture
Every line should answer four questions:
- What is this cost for?
- How much does the campaign expect this to require?
- When will this cost begin and when will it peak?
- Is this required, optional, or conditional on fundraising?

Those distinctions matter because the Funding Path Engine cannot responsibly tell the campaign what it needs to raise unless the plan itself is legible.

### Required vs Optional
Required lines are the costs the campaign is treating as core to the strategy. Optional lines are real possibilities but should not be allowed to quietly inflate the path as if they were locked. Conditional lines are especially important. These are items such as added field scale, extra polling, expanded digital, or video packages that may be strategically useful but should only go live if funding conditions are met.

### Priority language
Budget priority is not decorative. It is how the campaign should think when tradeoffs appear.

- **Core** means the campaign is not honestly running the intended strategy without it.
- **Important** means the campaign strongly prefers to fund it but could delay, reduce, or sequence it differently.
- **Conditional** means this cost becomes viable only if the path strengthens.
- **Stretch** means the campaign would like to do it, but it should not distort the plan.

### What good budgeting looks like
A strong budget plan has:
- every major domain represented,
- no major campaign function missing because “we’ll figure it out later,”
- realistic timing,
- notes on assumptions where uncertainty is high,
- and a clear distinction between must-have and nice-to-have.

### Common budgeting mistakes
The most common weak patterns are:
- underpricing staff and field ramp,
- ignoring event and finance program costs,
- treating reserve as an afterthought,
- assuming consultants are one-time instead of recurring,
- and leaving late persuasion or GOTV costs too vague to phase.

### How to use historical comparisons
The benchmark comparison on this page is there to improve realism, not to trap the campaign in an average. If the current plan is meaningfully above historical analogs, that does not automatically make it wrong. It means leadership should be able to explain why. If the current plan is far below historical analogs, leadership should be able to explain which strategic substitutes make that plausible.

## 3. Spend Timeline

### Manual title
**Spend Timeline — When Money Must Exist, Not Just How Much**

### Manual body
This page is about cash timing. Campaigns often fail not because they never raise meaningful money, but because the money arrives after the plan needed it.

The Spend Timeline takes the static budget and turns it into a calendar of pressure. It shows when spending starts, when it ramps, when large commitments hit, and when reserve stress becomes dangerous.

This page should be read as the finance equivalent of an operations calendar. It tells you where the campaign is likely to experience funding pressure before the pressure becomes visible in a crisis.

### Key interpretations

#### Monthly Spend Curve
This is the campaign’s planned burn pattern by month. A flat-looking curve suggests a steady model. A ramped curve suggests a campaign that scales. A sharp late peak suggests a campaign that is relying heavily on final-phase spending and therefore needs earlier reserve discipline.

#### Commitment Windows
A commitment window is the period when leadership needs money in hand or near-liquid before a major decision or contract becomes safe. Polls, media, field expansion, vendor deposits, and payroll growth often create commitment windows.

#### Peak Spend Window
This is the period in which the campaign’s cash stress is highest. It may be late, but it can also arrive earlier if staffing or field build begin before fundraising has matured.

#### Reserve Pressure
Reserve pressure is not the same thing as spending. A month can have moderate gross spending but still be dangerous if there is little cash cushion behind it.

### Practical reading guidance
This page matters most when the campaign is feeling tempted to say “we’ll raise it later.” If the timeline shows that the plan becomes fragile in August, then the relevant fundraising work often needs to happen in June and July, not August itself.

Strong finance leadership uses this page to protect decision quality. Weak finance leadership uses it only after the campaign has already overcommitted.

## 4. Historical Benchmarks

### Manual title
**Historical Benchmarks — What Similar Campaigns Actually Raised, Spent, and Carried**

### Manual body
This page exists to give realism and context to the active plan. It is not here to replace strategy or claim that every race should be run identically to its historical analogs.

The benchmark layer asks a simpler question: for races that look broadly similar to this one, what did serious campaigns usually raise, spend, and carry into key periods?

That matters for two reasons. First, it helps prevent budget plans and targets from floating free of reality. Second, it helps the campaign explain its own path to leadership, donors, consultants, or a candidate who needs historical grounding.

### How to read benchmark ranges
Benchmark ranges should never be treated as destiny. They are reference bands:
- a **floor** below which campaigns often struggle to build a serious operation,
- a **competitive threshold** where campaigns begin to look financially credible,
- and a **likely band** in which historically serious campaigns often operate.

### Important caution
A benchmark is not a substitute for the campaign’s actual operating plan. If the campaign intends to do more field, more digital, or more early infrastructure than comparable races, the plan may legitimately exceed the benchmark. The question is whether the campaign has a path to support that choice.

### Good use of this page
Use this page to ask:
- Are we planning something structurally heavier than the historical norm?
- Are we pretending we can win well below what credible campaigns usually bring together?
- Are we front-loading or back-loading differently than history suggests?
- Are we carrying enough reserve relative to similar campaigns?

## 5. Channel Plan

### Manual title
**Channel Plan — Where the Required Money Has to Come From**

### Manual body
This page translates the funding requirement into actual finance work. It takes the abstract target and allocates it across channels the campaign can control: call time, donor meetings, events, online, finance committee activity, and other major fundraising sources.

The point of this page is not to make channels look mathematically precise beyond what the campaign can know. The point is to stop the campaign from saying “we need to raise $150,000” without answering the practical follow-up: from where, by whom, and through what activity?

### What a healthy channel plan looks like
A healthy channel plan:
- matches the campaign’s actual strengths,
- spreads load across more than one source of production,
- protects against overdependence on a single event or donor cluster,
- and changes by phase.

A weak channel plan often hides wishful thinking. It assumes events will magically close the gap, treats online as a growth fantasy without evidence, or allocates too much weight to activity that the candidate is not actually going to complete.

### How to interpret channel warnings
Warnings on this page are not saying a channel is bad. They are saying the current plan may be too dependent, too weakly supported, or too far below requirement.

Examples:
- Event plan too dependent on one host universe.
- Candidate call time target below modeled requirement.
- Online share too optimistic relative to prior performance.
- Finance committee production concentrated in too few members.

## 6. Finance Operations

### Manual title
**Finance Operations — The Work That Must Happen for the Money to Arrive**

### Manual body
This page is where the campaign shifts from planning to execution. A funding path has no value if the campaign cannot translate it into real asks, meetings, events, follow-up, and deposits.

Finance Operations should feel like a disciplined production system, not a loose calendar. The question is not simply what is on the schedule. The question is whether the scheduled work is enough, whether it happened, what it produced, and what still requires follow-up.

### Why this page matters
Campaigns often believe they are “working the finance plan” because there are many meetings on the calendar or because the candidate feels busy. This page forces a stricter standard:
- Were the right activities scheduled?
- Did they happen?
- Did they generate asks, pledges, and received dollars?
- Was follow-up completed?
- Did that production match the path requirement?

### What to watch
Look for:
- call time hours versus plan,
- ask count versus plan,
- pledge conversion,
- event net yield,
- donor follow-up backlog,
- and the extent to which actual work is carrying the modeled requirement.

## 7. Donor Intelligence

### Manual title
**Donor Intelligence — Who Is Funding the Campaign and From Where**

### Manual body
This page explains the composition of the donor base. It is not a vanity map and it is not a sociology exercise detached from campaign use. It exists to help the campaign understand whether its financial coalition is broad, narrow, local, external, professional-heavy, service-heavy, repeat-dependent, or structurally fragile.

This matters because the source of money affects both viability and strategy. A campaign whose money is concentrated in a small number of outside professional networks is in a different condition than a campaign drawing broad but lower-dollar support from within the district.

### Important caution
Occupation and geography summaries should be read as structured approximations, not total social truth. Use them to identify patterns, not to make overconfident claims about every donor.

### Practical uses
This page should inform:
- host strategy,
- finance committee recruitment,
- event placement,
- donor development priorities,
- and risk messaging about overdependence.

## 8. Expenditure Intelligence

### Manual title
**Expenditure Intelligence — What the Campaign Is Actually Buying, and Whether It Fits the Plan**

### Manual body
This page exists because campaigns often focus so hard on raising money that they lose discipline over what the money is becoming once spent.

The purpose of this page is to compare the campaign’s active or reported spend profile against:
- the approved budget,
- strategic priorities,
- and the historical shape of similar races.

This page should be used to identify drift. A campaign that says field is central but is spending disproportionately on consulting, admin, or one-off production costs should see that early.

### What to watch
- consultant share,
- overhead share,
- field share,
- event cost efficiency,
- variance from budget,
- and spend categories that are crowding out more important needs.

## 9. Risk Console

### Manual title
**Risk Console — Where the Finance Plan Is Becoming Fragile**

### Manual body
This page is the campaign’s early warning surface. It should not exist only to make danger feel dramatic. It should exist to help leadership act before fragility becomes failure.

The best way to use this page is not to scan for red cards and feel alarmed. Instead, identify:
- what triggered the warning,
- what assumption changed,
- what operation or budget area is exposed,
- and what action would materially reduce the risk.

### Risk categories
The major risk families are:
- pace risk,
- reserve risk,
- budget pressure,
- field funding risk,
- channel dependence,
- donor concentration,
- activity completion risk,
- pledge conversion risk,
- historical realism risk.

### What the page should teach
Every risk should answer:
- Why is this a risk now?
- What part of the campaign does it threaten?
- What evidence supports the flag?
- What is the immediate corrective action?

## 10. Reports

### Manual title
**Reports — Turning Engine Outputs Into Campaign Action**

### Manual body
Reports are not administrative byproducts. They are one of the main reasons the engine exists. A good report should help a candidate, finance director, manager, consultant, or finance committee member understand what matters now without digging through the app.

The report pack should never feel generic. Each report should:
- state the funding condition clearly,
- explain the main drivers,
- identify the most important risks,
- and recommend next actions.

### What a weak report looks like
A weak report is full of summary language but light on meaning. It restates numbers without interpreting them, uses consultant-sounding filler, or hides risk behind polite phrasing.

### What a strong report looks like
A strong report gives the reader:
- the condition,
- the explanation,
- the consequence,
- and the next move.

## 11. Manual / Help Page

### Manual title
**How to Use CFE — Reading the Finance Engine Like an Operator**

### Manual body
This manual is not a glossary alone. It is a working guide to how campaign finance planning should be read inside this system.

The correct way to use CFE is:
1. define the campaign you are actually trying to run,
2. phase when the costs hit,
3. understand what history says is credible,
4. generate the funding path,
5. allocate responsibility by channel,
6. track whether the work actually happened,
7. review what the money and activity are telling you,
8. adjust the plan before the campaign is forced to adjust it under stress.

The app is strongest when the campaign uses it as a management instrument rather than as a retrospective summary tool.
