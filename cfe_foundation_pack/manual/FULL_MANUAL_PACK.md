# Campaign Finance Engine (CFE) — Full Manual Pack

## Manual front matter
Campaign Finance Engine is built for campaigns that need to fund real plans, not admire spreadsheets. The purpose of the system is to help leadership understand what the campaign is trying to fund, when those costs hit, whether history suggests the plan is realistic, and what the finance operation must do next to support it.

The system is strongest when it is used honestly. A realistic budget is more valuable than a flattering one. A visible unknown is better than a false label. A warning that causes a course correction is more useful than a perfect-looking dashboard that hides pressure until it is too late.

## How to read the system
Every major output in CFE belongs to one of three classes:
- Reported: directly reflected from source data or direct user input
- Standardized: normalized or grouped by system rules
- Modeled: calculated by the engine from the selected plan, timing, and assumptions

A serious operator should always know which kind of output they are looking at.

## What “good” looks like in CFE
Good use of CFE does not mean every status stays green. Good use means:
- the budget is real
- the timing is visible
- the path is understandable
- the activity is disciplined
- the reports are circulation-ready
- the campaign knows what to do next

## Module-by-module operator notes

### Overview
Use the overview to orient, not to think. It should tell the user what deserves attention first: path status, reserve status, next checkpoint, field funding status, and active risks.

### Budget
The budget should not be treated as a wish list. It should be treated as a timed set of commitments. Separate what is required from what is merely desired.

### Timeline
The timeline is where abstract budget numbers become real campaign pressure. If the campaign is going to feel squeezed, the timeline usually shows it before the checking account does.

### Benchmarks
Benchmarks are there to discipline assumptions. They are not there to erase campaign-specific context.

### Funding Path
This is the engine’s core answer. When leadership asks, “What do we need to raise and by when?” this is where that answer should come from.

### Channels
Channel planning should be diversified enough that the campaign does not depend on one heroic assumption.

### Activity
The campaign should be able to open the activity layer and know exactly what finance work is due, who owns it, and what result it is expected to produce.

### Pledges
Treat pledges and receipts as different truths. A pledge is political information. A receipt is operating reality.

### Donors
Donor intelligence is most useful when used for strategy rather than vanity. The right question is not just who gave. It is what that pattern means for growth, concentration, and coalition depth.

### Spending
The spending layer should help leadership see whether actual disbursements match the campaign’s stated strategic posture.

### Risks
Risks should be read as decision prompts. A warning is not an accusation. It is a sign that timing, concentration, or execution is becoming harder to ignore.

### Reports
Reports should be ready to circulate with minimal editing. The software should already sound like a serious campaign operation.

## Healthy ranges and interpretation guidance
Because campaigns vary, CFE should avoid fake-universal thresholds in the manual. Instead, it should speak in comparative language:
- healthier than planned
- in range
- tighter than preferred
- materially behind
- unusually concentrated
- heavier than the historical norm

## Common operator mistakes to guard against
- entering vague budget lines that hide real commitments
- assuming pledges are the same as receipts
- treating benchmarks as destiny
- ignoring reserve pressure because the topline feels acceptable
- assuming events will solve structural underperformance
- failing to revisit channel mix when one source softens
- delaying follow-up on live commitments
